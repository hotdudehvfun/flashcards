Features of Rights

1. Some are for Citizens only

a.      Art. 15 16 19 29 30 are only for citizens

2. Not sacrosanct or permanent

3. Curtail or repeal them but only by a constitutional amendment act

4. Articles 20 and 21 cannot be suspended

5. Article 19 can be suspended only on grounds of External Rebellion

6. Scope of operation is limited by

a.      Article 31A (saving of laws providing for acquisition of estates, etc.)

b.      Article 31B (validation of certain acts and regulations included in the 9th Schedule)

c.      Article 31C (saving of laws giving effect to certain Directive Principles).

7. Article 33: Paramilitary forces restricted or abrogated by the Parliament

8. Article 34: Martial Law

9. Article 35: Law can be made only by the Parliament and not by state legislatures so that uniformity throughout the country is maintained

 

State

Article 12: States contains

1.      Parliament

2.      State Legislatives

3.      All local bodies panchayat, municipality

4.      Statutory and non-statutory bodies

5.      Note: Supreme Court, even a private body or an agency working as an instrument of the State falls within the meaning of the State under Article 12

Fundamental Rights facts and exceptions

Right to Equality( 14-18)

1. Equality before law is of British origin

2. Equal protection of laws has been taken from the American.

3. Source of rights is constitution

4. Exception:

             i.     Article 31C can be used for DPSP and Article 14 can be curtail

            ii.     Protection of President, Governor, Ministers etc.

5. Exceptions

             i.     Reservations

            ii.     SC ST , children, admission in institutes

6. Mandal Commission 1979

             i.     Morarji Desai Government appointed the Second Backward Classes Commission

            ii.     Investigate the conditions of the socially and educationally backward classes and suggest measures for their advancement

7. Mandal case (1992)

             i.     Article 16(4): provides for reservation of jobs in favour of backward classes, examined thoroughly by the Supreme Court.

            ii.     Court has rejected the additional reservation of 10% for poorer sections of higher castes

          iii.     Upheld the constitutional validity of 27% reservation for the OBCs with certain conditions

          iv.     Creamy layer should be excluded from reservation

            v.     No reservation in promotions

          vi.     Reserved quota should not exceed 50% except in some extraordinary situations

         vii.     A permanent statutory body should be established to examine complaints of over-inclusion and under-inclusion in the list of OBCs. With regard to the above rulings of the Supreme Court, the government has taken the following actions:

        viii.     Ram Nandan Committee:

1. Appointed to Identify the creamy layer among the OBCs.

2. National Commission for Backward Classes was established in 1993 by an act of Parliament

3. 77th Amendment Act 1995

a. Nullify reservation in promotions

b. Article 16: added State to provide for reservation in promotions of any services under the State in favour of the SCs and STs that are not adequately represented in the state services

4. 85th Amendment Act 2001

a. provides for consequential seniority in the case of promotion by virtue of rule of reservation for the government servants

          ix.     81st Amendment 2000

1. Article 16: added State to consider the unfilled reserved vacancies of a year as a separate class of vaccancies to be filled up in any succeeding year or years.

2. Such class of vacancies are not to be combined with the vacancies of the year in which they are being filled up to determine the ceiling of 50% reservation on total number of vacancies of that year.

3. In brief, it ends the 50% ceiling on reservation in backlog vacancies.

            x.     76th Amendment Act of 1994

1. Tamil Nadu Reservations Act 1994 in the Ninth Schedule to protect it from judicial review as it provided for 69% of reservation, far exceeding the 50% ceiling.

8. Article 17: UNTOUCHABILITY

             i.     Not defined in the Constitution

            ii.     Does not cover social boycott of a few individuals or their exclusion from religious services, etc

          iii.     person convicted of the offence of untouchability is disqualified for election to the Parliament or state legislature

9. Article 18: Abolition of Titles

             i.     state cannot conferee any title whether a citizen or a foreigner

            ii.     Prohibits a citizen of India from accepting any title from any foreign state

          iii.     A foreigner holding any office of profit or trust under the state cannot accept any title from any foreign state without the consent of the president

          iv.     No citizen or foreigner holding any office of profit or trust under the State is to accept any present, emolument or office from or under any foreign State without the consent of the president

            v.     National Awards :1954

1. Are not titles

2. But cannot be used as prefix or suffix

3. Janata Party government headed by Morarji Desai discontinued them in 1977

4. Again revived in 1980 by the Indira Gandhi government

 

Right to freedom(Article 19,20,21,22)

 

1. Speech, assembly, reside, profession, association, move freely

2. Right to acquire land deleted and inserted in 300-A by 44th Amendment 1978

3. Protects only from state but not from private

4. SC held that the trade unions have no guaranteed right to effective bargaining or right to strike or right to declare a lock-out. The right to strike can be controlled by an appropriate industrial law

5. Article 19: Right to move inside the country

6. Article 21: Right to move out of the country and right to come back to the country

7. Gopalan case (1950)

1. the Supreme Court has taken a narrow interpretation of the Article 21

8. Menaka case (1978)

1. Supreme Court overruled its judgement in the Gopalan case by taking a wider interpretation of the Article 21

Protection Against Arrest and Detention

1. Article 22

1. Grants protection to persons who are arrested or detained

2. Detention types

a. Punitive

                             i.     Punitive detention is to punish a person for an offence committed by him after trial and conviction in a court

                            ii.     safeguards are not available to an enemy alien or a person arrested or detained under a preventive detention law

b. Preventive

                             i.     Detention of a person without trial and conviction by a court

                            ii.     detention of a person cannot exceed three months

3. Article 22 also authorises the Parliament to prescribe

a. circumstances and the classes of cases in which a person can be detained for more than three months

b. maximum period for which a person can be detained in any classes of cases under a preventive detention

4. 44th Amendment Act of 1978

a. reduced the period of detention without obtaining the opinion of an advisory board from three to two months

b. but not operational

5. Double jeopardy

6. Available only in proceedings before a court of law or a judicial tribunal

7. Not available in proceedings before departmental or administrative authorities as they are not of judicial nature

Cultural Rights(Article 29,30)

 

Article 29

1. provides that any section of the citizens residing in any part of India having a distinct language, script or culture of its own, shall have the right to conserve the same

 

Article 30

1. Grants the following rights to minorities, whether religious or linguistic

a. Right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice

b. Compensation amount fixed by the State for the compulsory acquisition of any property of a minority educational institution shall not restrict or abrogate the right guaranteed to them.

                     i.     Added by the 44th Amendment Act of 1978 to protect the right of minorities in this regard. The Act deleted the right to property as a Fundamental Right (Article 31). (c) In granting aid, the State shall not discriminate against any educational institution managed by a minority.

c. granting aid, the State shall not discriminate against any educational institution managed by a minority

2. Article 30 is confined only to minorities (religious or linguistic) and does not extend to any section of citizens (as under Article 29).

3. Right of a minority to impart education to its children in its own language.

4. Minority educational institutions

a. recognition as well as aid: State

b. only recognition: State

c. neither recognition nor aid from the State: free to manage affairs

5. Secretary of Malankara Syrian Catholic College case: Institutional Rights of minorities

a. Choose its governing body

b. Appoint teaching staff

c. Admit eligible students and fee structure

d. Use assets for the benefit

e. Right is not absolute. Nor does it include the right to maladminister

f. Regulations made by the State do not in any manner interfere with the right under Article 30(1)(rights of minorities)

g. Unaided minority educational institutions will have the freedom to appoint teachers/lecturers by adopting any rational procedure

h. Extension of aid by the State does not alter the nature and character of the minority educational institutions. The conditions can be imposed by the State to ensure proper utilization of the aid, without however diluting or abridging the right under Article 30(1).

Martial Law: Article 34

 

a.      restrictions on fundamental rights while martial law is in force in any area within the territory of India

b.      Act of Indemnity made by the Parliament cannot be challenged in any court on the ground of contravention of any of the fundamental rights

d.      military authorities are vested with abnormal powers to take all necessary steps.

EXCEPTIONS TO FUNDAMENTAL RIGHT

a.      Article 31A

                 i.     Saves five categories of laws from being challenged

                ii.     Does not immunise a state law from judicial review unless it has been reserved for the presidents consideration and has received his assent

 

b.     Article 31B

                 i.     saves the acts and regulations included in the Ninth Schedule

                ii.     I.R. Coelho case (2007)

1.      SC ruled that there could not be any blanket immunity from judicial review of laws included in the Ninth Schedule

c.      Article 31C

                 i.     25th Amendment Act of 1971

1.      DPSP>FR in case of Article 39b and 39c even if it violates Article 14 and Article 19

2.      Not up for Judicial Review

                ii.     Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)

1.      SC said 2nd provision is not valid

              iii.     42nd Amendment Act (1976)

1.      Extended the scope of the above first provision of Article 31C by including within its protection any law to implement any of the directive principles specified in Part IV of the Constitution and not merely in Article 39 (b) or (c)

              iv.     Minerva Mills case (1980)

1.      But again declared Invalid by SC
