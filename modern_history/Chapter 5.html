Chapter 5 Structure of Govt policiesEconomic policies 1757-1857

After acquiring vats empire, EIC had to come up with devices to control administration.
Otherwise they will be replace by some king or prince or other foreign company

Moreover, back home in Britain merchants and rich traders were growing jealous of EIC
they also wanted share in fabulous wealth of Bengal

1765, EIC won battle of Buxar
Mughals, Nawabs were virtually on feet of EIC
EIC got diwani rights in 1765
Clive started Dual Government system: 1765-1772
Indians got responsibility to run administration 
EIC got all the rights to collect revenue and taxes with no liability

1772:Dual govt. ended after enacting series of Acts 
1773 Regulating Act 
  1. First step to control EIC
  2. COD to report on civil, military and revenues to ministries in England
  3. post of governor was upgraded to gov-gen with council.
  4. gov-gen is to work as agent of Britain's govt.
  5. Bombay and Madras were to be under Bengal
  6. SC at Calcutta
But Regulating Act failed
No effective control over EIC administration
Council could out vote gov-gen on any matter, result deadlocks were common
no effective control over madras, Bombay
Due to all this another act 1784

1784 Pitt's Act
  1. Main: BOC with 6 commissioner including 2 cabinet ministers
  2. Board of control took over political matters
  3. control over military, civil, revenue
  4. COD remained as commercial body
  5. Now gov-gen became superior, even if one council member vote in his favor, he can have his way
1786: gov. gen. can overrule council

Charter Act 1813:
Monopoly of EIC ended
but with china for tea and trade was still exclusive to EIC

Charter Act 1833
  1. ended tea and trade monopoly of EIC with China
  2. made EIC purely administrative body, no commercial
  3. Bombay and madras were stripped of legislative powers
  4. Gov-Gen upgraded to Gov.Gen of India
  5. Debts of company taken over by govt.
  6. company to run under BOC
No Indians in administrations
Three seats with supreme control
  1. BOC represent British govt.
  2. COD of company
  3. Gov.Gen of India with his council
Economic Policies of Company
  1. Commercial Policy
    1. 1600-1757 EIC was purely commercial

    3. profits came from sale of Indians goods in foreign market
    4. After 1757: company used political powers to push its trade
    5. company used political power to force weavers to sell cheap and buy dear
    6.  
  2. Drain of Wealth
    1. Started after 1757, EIC servants began to hoard wealth and took home immense fortune
  3. Transport and Communication
    1. 1839 started Grant Trunk Road and finished in 1850
    2. Rapid railway development under Lord Dalhousie 1849
    3. proposed network of four main trunk lines to link interior of country
    4. all money invested by govt.
    5. first 50 years financially losing concern
    6. first telegraph line 1853: Calcutta to Agra
    7. Dalhousie introduced stamps 
  4. Land Revenue Policy
    1. Permanent Settlement :Cornwallis 1793
      1. Place: Bengal, Bihar,orissa, north district of madras, varanasi
      2. Revenue collections through Zamindars
      3. Rate: 10/11 to govt. 1/11 to Zamindars
      4. out of 100 govt. took 45, zamindars got 15 and cultivators left with 40
      5. Right of ownership was made hereditary
      6. Zamindar was landlord over tenant and subordinate  to govt.
      7. But landlord in britain was owner to tenant and also in relation to state
    2. Ryotwari Settlement: Munro 1820
      1. Place: South and South Western india
      2. Rent: upto 45-55 percent
      3. govt. adopted ryotwari because no big zamindars
      4. settlement not permanent: revised in 20-30 years
      5. peasants were never owners of lands, they just had to pay RENT else their land will be sold to others to recover debt.
    3. Mahalwari System: Bentinck 1833
      1. Places: Northern parts of India, Punjab, Gangetic Valley, North-West Provinces
      2. Collection through whole villages
      3.  Mahalwari system had many provisions of both the Zamindari System and Ryotwari System.
      4. In this system, the land was divided into Mahals. Each Mahal comprises one or more villages.
      5. Ownership rights were vested with the peasants.
      6. The villages committee was held responsible for collection of the taxes.
